The Protein Folding Challenge

Predicting the 3D structure of proteins remains one of the hardest problems in computational biology — NP‑complete even in simplified models. While deep learning (AlphaFold) and citizen science (FoldIT) have made tremendous progress, many cases (intrinsically disordered proteins, membrane proteins, metalloenzymes) still require quantum‑aware energy evaluations and human intuition.

qFoldIT is an open platform that unifies three complementary approaches: DeepFoldIT (neural network initializer), Rosetta@Home (classical Metropolis), and qFold (quantum‑accelerated Metropolis). Gamification invites thousands of players to contribute, while AAA‑grade visualization (Unigine 2 Sim) makes molecular interactions tangible.

The platform is built on Nobel‑prize winning discoveries and is entirely free for academic use.

Technical Architecture

qFoldIT integrates three complementary computational engines:

  • DeepFoldIT — a deep residual network (inspired by AlphaFold) predicts torsion angles (φ, ψ) and distances, generating initial conformations.
  • Rosetta@Home — classical Metropolis Monte Carlo with the Rosetta energy function performs local optimisation on CPU clusters.
  • qFold — a coined Szegedy quantum walk (quantum Metropolis) that provides quadratic speedup for rugged energy landscapes. It runs on simulators (Qiskit‑Aer) or real quantum backends (IonQ, D‑Wave, Rosatom).

Quantum adapter — unified API to multiple QPUs. Microservices (bio‑ml, rosetta, quantum‑adapter, autoresearch, animation) are containerised (Docker) and orchestrated by Kubernetes on Amazon EKS.

Visualisation — Unigine 2 Sim (64‑bit double precision, GPU‑based Gaussian splatting, volumetric raymarching) renders molecules with cinematic quality at 60+ fps, allowing seamless zoom from Arctic landscapes to atomic orbitals.

Interactive Real‑Time Engine: NanoVer Server

To bring true real‑time interactivity and multi‑user collaborative VR to qFoldIT, we have integrated NanoVer Server — originally developed for interactive molecular dynamics (iMD) in virtual reality (NanoVer ecosystem, JOSS 2025). NanoVer Server acts as the central iMD orchestrator, enabling:

  • Live force feedback — Players can “grab” atoms, apply forces in real time, and see the molecular response within milliseconds. The server executes OpenMM steps between frame publications while continuously updating iMD forces.
  • Quantitatively accurate iMD — Every user‑induced perturbation is recorded (collective forces, potential energy, cumulative work done), making the gamified exploration fully reproducible and citable in scientific publications.
  • Multi‑user colocated/distributed VR — Multiple researchers wearing Meta Quest headsets (via NanoVer iMD‑VR client) or using desktop Unigine clients can share the same virtual molecular environment, see each other’s avatars, and collaboratively fold proteins from different physical locations.
  • Jupyter‑native analysis — NanoVer’s Python client connects directly to the same server, allowing real‑time plotting of energies, trajectories, and forces inside Jupyter notebooks. This bridges gamification with data science.
  • Hybrid classical‑quantum pipeline — OpenMM (integrated with NanoVer Server) provides instant classical response for low‑latency gameplay, while the qFold quantum adapter runs as a background microservice to refine energy landscapes and suggest optimal moves. NanoVer Server seamlessly switches between engines.
NanoVer Server — interactive molecular dynamics engine powering qFoldIT’s real‑time multiplayer VR iMD · OpenMM · gRPC

The communication protocol (gRPC/WebSocket) is language‑agnostic, enabling our Unigine 2 Sim client (C++) to exchange frames and forces with the Python‑based NanoVer Server. The server runs as a containerised microservice alongside the quantum‑adapter and Rosetta modules, orchestrated by Kubernetes. This architecture transforms qFoldIT from a batch‑oriented platform into a live, interactive, multiplayer molecular playground where scientific discovery and gamification feed each other in real time.

Open source commitment: NanoVer Server is freely available under CC BY 4.0, and all qFoldIT‑specific adapters will be released on GitHub. Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) and Yonsei University have already joined as collaborators to co‑develop the integrated platform.

Quantum Training Module: Cold Atom Lab (NASA/DLR)

From city‑farms and microscopes to space‑grade quantum hardware — qFoldIT extends its digital‑twin philosophy to the Cold Atom Lab (CAL) aboard the ISS. CAL creates Bose‑Einstein condensates, “giant atoms” that make quantum effects visible on a macroscopic scale. It is the first quantum laboratory in microgravity, achieving seconds‑long coherence times impossible on Earth.

DLR / horizons — Cold Atom Lab: Creating “giant atoms” to observe quantum effects at macroscopic scale Destiny module · ISS

In qFoldIT, the CAL module serves as a high‑fidelity training simulator — a direct evolution of the platform’s proven ability to control real‑world equipment (lamps, pumps, microscopes) from virtual environments. Players step into the role of a quantum payload operator:

  • Interactive assembly and calibration — Align laser beams, tune magnetic traps, and replace optical modules following holographic guides (inspired by the HoloLens 2 procedure performed by astronaut Megan McArthur in 2021).
  • Live BEC visualisation — Observe the formation of a Bose‑Einstein condensate in real time. The cloud appears as a bright, coherent spot; its lifetime depends on the player's precision in minimising environmental noise.
  • Telemetry dashboard — Realistic data streams (atom number, temperature, phase‑space density) mirror those seen by operators at NASA JPL, teaching players to interpret the “language” of a quantum sensor.
  • Cross‑platform scalability — From simplified 2D cooling puzzles (Dendy‑style) to full Unigine 2 SIM VR with volumetric laser beams and tactile feedback via NanoVer.

Scientific and educational value: The CAL module transforms qFoldIT into a workforce‑development tool for the quantum industry. Students and early‑career researchers gain hands‑on experience with ultra‑cold atom hardware that is physically inaccessible (CAL is the only facility of its kind on orbit). The module aligns with qFoldIT’s core philosophy — using gamification to make cutting‑edge science intuitive, accessible, and playable.
